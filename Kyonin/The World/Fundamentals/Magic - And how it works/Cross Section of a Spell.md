### This is how magic is broken down from the perspective of wizards within the world of [[Kyonin]]:

# What makes up a given spell?
Spells are broken up into several "pieces". Each of these pieces is **important**, and *necessary*\* for a spell to "succeed". - (Meaning it'll have the desired affect once the casting is complete)
- The fundamental's of these "pieces" acted as the blueprint for [[firearms]] when they were invented.

tab: #### 1. [[Sigils]] - The Body
Sigil is a general use term for a drawing, carving, etching, etc. used to evoke some form of spell craft.
- **e.g.**, A transmutation circle. Protective wards. Nercromantic seal.
---

The most important piece to any good *sigil* is the substance used to create it's many parts. With that being said however, there isn't one singular "**best**" option. Most of the time a spellcaster will use whatever is available to them, and then adjust the remaining pieces of the spell to achieve their desired affect. Throughout the planes of existence there are *thousands* of different medium's for the creation of sigils, but millage may vary.
- **e.g.**, You may want to cast a spell that uses a type of wood and a being's blood as material components. You could try any of the following and achieve your desired affect.
	- Carve the sigil into a tree and then paint over the sigil with the blood of the given being to meet both the structural requirements of the spell as well as the material components.
	- Paint the sigil on the ground with the being's blood and simply supply the wood as the material required, either in the sigil or held on your person.
	- Write the sigil out in ink within a spell book on your person, and then supply the the materials required.

tab: ##### [[Symbols]]
To start, first, we must address that "Symbols" is a colloquial term for any kind of message, whether coded or not, written within a sigil. 
The astute among you may ask: "Won't that mess up a spells affect? Why would you want to write messages within a sigil?". Put simply, no it won't ruin a spell, but there are a few caveats.
- If you plan to use the same substance to create the main lines and shapes of the sigil and whatever symbolic message you're looking to write out, you **MUST** make sure that the symbolic message does **NOT** touch the rest of the sigil. This can and will destroy, or reshape, a spell.
- If you're preforming any kind of ritual spell with a "consumption circle" make sure that the rune that triggers the consumption is the only marking within the circle. 

But why add a symbols to begin with? What's the point? Well adding symbols to a sigil is an ancient tradition going back to the origins of spell craft. In the early days of spell craft those first casters needed a way to communicate to other casters what they'd discovered about one of the aspects of a spell without the general public, or rival casters, learning dangerous secrets of spell craft. So they created and used many symbolic languages, each devised by different factions of spellcasters.

Today these symbols are mainly used by the novice spellcaster, to learn what to do and not to do with a given spell, and master spellcasters for coding and hiding the intent of their spells.

tab: ##### [[Runes]]
Runes is general use term designed to be a "catch-all" term for any of the alchemical, religious, and demonic runes used within the more intricate parts of a sigil, like a consumption circle, *i.e.*, a designated spot for material components to be consumed by a spell by envoking some element or higher power.
- **e.g.**, Putting the runes for projection, `♓︎`, and, multiplication, `♒︎`, is one of the core components of the spell **scorching ray**. But by adding a *consumption circle* around the main spell, with the rune for air, `🜁`, surrounded by the rune for fire, `🜂`, and multiplication, around both the main spell and new consumptions circle, you'll cast it at a "higher level" and multiply the number of rays, but also increase the amount air needed to cast.

tab: ##### [[Shapes]]
A great many, if not most, novices will ask why certain shapes are used to encompass and surround spells. Like why use a circle when an oval will give us more room? Or why use a square when a diamond shape is more appealing visually. Put simply practicality.
To use shapes outside of the normal convention isn't unheard of, and honestly most mages go through a phase where they draw their sigils more askew, but rarely do mages continue this trend after working with other mages for any length of time. It becomes impractical to change up a colleges spell simply to meet ones own aesthetics. 
- However a great deal of mages make a good deal of money altering a spells sigil so that the spell will look a certain way, like altering **Mage Armor** so that it resembles the armor of a city lords legion, or modifying **Find Familiar** so that the summoned familiar resembles an old family pet.
- To that end, there has also been a great deal of "sacrilegious" research done to swap out the standard, predefined looks of religous spells. To help differintiate a cleric from their order.

With the matter of aesthetics addressed, the reason we use shapes at all is, funny enough, to shape our spells. Take for example **Shield**. The outer most shape, of the most basic version, is hexagonal because:
1. The nature of the spell requires the spell to be rigid like the lines of a hexagon. 
2. When replecated over and over again the hexagon is the perfect shape to create an interlocking expandable grid of protection.

tab: ##### Overview
>[!warning]- 
>Contrary to popular belief magic does *NOT* create things out of nothing. Wether it be a litteral component, a contract, an oath, or even ones very own soul. There is always some form of cost. I advise a great deal of caution when casting any spell who's cost you're not explicitly familiar with. Look for a *consumption circle* in every new spell you come across, then identify the *Rune* in the center of the circle so you'll know what the base cost is. If there is no *consumption circle* that you can decern, then *DO NOT* cast it! There is no telling what the cost is, and a great deal of celestial and demonic entities have used "free spells" to ensnare foolish mages.
Much like every other part of the spell the components determine the kind of spell you're trying to cast. A few examples:
- All fire spells require air in order to fuel.
- Water spells can't be cast in an environment that is too dry.
- Some spells require a tax to use since they're owned by certain individulas.
  - **e.g.** **Jim's Magic Missle**
- Spells to create armor require material to work from, such as leather.
  
Fret not, you needn't carry around massive containers filled to the brim with all manner of components so that you're ready for every situation that comes your way. Instead you can simply do as most modern spell casters do and store your components within your grimoire by turning it into a *casting focus*.

#### *Spellcasting Focuses*
A *Spellcasting Focus*, or simply focus, is any item that can hold the rune for destruction, and storage within a consumption circle, known as the **Sigil of Focus**. Without getting too technical the sigil of focus does exaclty what the runes it's comprised of says they do, it destroys and then stores components in a micro poket of reality to be used whenever a mage casts a spell that requires a given component.

tab: ##### Overview
As we near the end of this quick glance into spellcraft the concepts become more simplistic, this is because these last two concepts are more physical and are used to aim and initiate a spell. With that being said they're both very interesting pieces.

Somatics are used to guide spells. Plain and simple. Most modern sigils are designed so that if a somatic gesture isn't used the spell will "fizzle out" and do nothing. This has been a hot topic for debate, since it makes mages even easier to catch and imprison, however it also means that a **Fireball** isn't sent careening in a random direction when cast while distracted.

Somatics are also a very important element to pay attetion to if you want to duel a mage. They'll often tell you where a spell is going and how many of a projectile is heading in that direction.

tab: ##### Triggers
Triggers are, put most simply, what innitiates a spell. In order to cast the **Firebolt** cantrip you simply need the corresponding sigil, to point at object of your ire, and say *ignis*, pronouced: *ig-nǝs-*. This will cause the spell to trigger and subsequently hit your target. Most spells require a verbal trigger to cast, which is why the spell **Silence** is such an effective counter to most traditional mages.

#### Detonations
There are rarer and more advanced spells that require both a trigger and detonation phrase, like the spell **Delayed Blast Fireball**. In order to cast these spells you'll also need a boatload more concentration and patience, but be warned, regardless if you lose concentration for just a moment the spell **WILL NOT** activate unless it's detonation phrase is uttered, or if the spell is illegaly altered.
